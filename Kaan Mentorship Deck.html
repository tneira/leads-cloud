Two Purchasing Structures

CNU and NetCredit both use two mechanisms to buy leads. The volume mix and cost structure differ significantly between the two brands.

Structure comparison
StructureIndustry nameCost structureCNU mixNC mix
SmartBidding PingTree Cost per Lead — paid upfront regardless of funding ~70% of volume Small / new
Offer Wall Online marketplace Cost per Funded — risk on lead provider ~30% of volume Primary channel
SmartBidding risk
Cost is paid when the lead is purchased, not when it funds. Decreases in loan conversion raise cost per funded directly. The conversion risk sits entirely with CNU.
Offer Wall risk
CNU pays only when a loan funds. But if revenue per click drops significantly, the lead provider can turn CNU off for that traffic. Volume risk rather than conversion risk.
The inverse mix between brands is the most interesting feature of the channel. CNU has optimized into SmartBidding at scale. NC has the opposite exposure and the largest gap to close.

The PingTree Auction

The PingTree is an auction system where lenders are prioritized by willingness to pay. CNU gets first look, and every loss provides a free market data point.

How the cascade works
1
Price ordering — CNU at the top. Lenders willing to bid the most are placed first and get first look. Due to volume, CNU typically holds this position and underwrites the application before any competitor.
2
Lead provider evaluates the bid. It either accepts or shows the application to lenders with a lower willingness to pay that is still above the bid it received.
3
Cascade continues downward. The lead provider goes down in price until it finds a buyer above prior bids, or accepts one of those earlier bids.
4
On a loss, free price discovery. CNU participates in a second-price sealed auction. When we lose a bid, we receive the winning bid amount. Every loss is a labeled data point about market clearing prices.
This is the foundation for a clearing-price model.
Auction mechanics
Second-price sealed bid. Price-ordered, sequential. First bidder to clear the reserve wins.

CNU's advantage. Volume gives us first look on every lead before lower-positioned lenders see it.

On losses. The winning bid is revealed. We observe market price even without winning. This is rare in auction markets.

CNU SmartBidding Model

CNU underwrites the application and submits a bid based on three factors. This is a sophisticated, production-grade system — the benchmark NC needs to reach.

The three-factor bid
Bid = f ( Customer LTV ) x g ( Conversion likelihood ) ÷ Market price
Customer LTV
Credit report data and application data that allow projection of customer profitability. The most mature component — reuses existing underwriting infrastructure.
Conversion likelihood
Signals from the lead provider, requested loan amount, and other indicators of lower likelihood the customer will take out the loan. The highest-leverage input for controlling CPF.
Market price
Second-price sealed auction: when we lose, we receive the winning bid. This gives real-time price discovery on losses — a labeled dataset for modeling market clearing prices.
The efficiency and the risk are the same thing. The SmartBidding program is highly efficient at purchasing leads. But cost is paid upfront on a cost-per-lead basis, so decreases in loan conversion translate directly into increased cost per funded with no safety valve.

Offer Wall Structure

Traditional online marketplace where all offers presented to the customer are shown simultaneously. Risk sits with the lead provider, not CNU.

How Offer Wall works
All lender offers are displayed side-by-side. The customer chooses. The lead provider benefits by giving customers multiple options — the expectation is that at least one will fund. Because of this, cost is charged per funded loan, not per lead.
CNU's position on the Offer Wall
CNU is less competitive on the Offer Wall because its offer terms are not as strong as competitors. However, CNU is often the only lender that provides an offer, and in those sessions it can be a strong addition to an Offer Wall portfolio.
Volume risk on Offer Wall
The main risk is not conversion — it is volume. If CNU's revenue per click drops significantly, the lead provider may turn CNU off for that traffic segment.

This is less common than conversion risk in SmartBidding, but Offer Wall requires monitoring RPC alongside CPF.
The strategic use case. Offer Wall captures near-prime and subprime segments that would not see a CNU offer elsewhere. The sessions where CNU is the only lender are the most diagnostic for understanding true demand.

NetCredit: The Inverse Structure

NC has the same two purchasing structures as CNU, but the volume is inverse. The SmartBidding program is still in infancy with a naive bidding model that creates two structural problems.

NC's current posture
NC primarily operates in Offer Walls and manages CPF campaigns, in part due to high sensitivity to spend volatility. SmartBidding was recently launched but remains early-stage.
High sensitivity to spend volatility means NC prioritizes the CPF cost structure of Offer Wall over the upfront-cost risk of SmartBidding. Conservative but volume-limiting.
NC SmartBidding: naive model
Current bidding strategy
Bids driven entirely by underwriting model score (1 to 10):

Score 1 $20  ·  Score 2 $18  ·  Score 3 $16  ·  ...

Top bid is capped at $20.
Two structural problems with the naive model
Problem 1 — The $20 ceiling. NC only sees traffic when no other lender bids at or above $20. The entire high-value segment of the PingTree is invisible to NC. It competes only for leads that no one else wants.
Problem 2 — Conversion drag. NC requires more verification on each lead, creating more customer drop-off. The naive bidding model does not account for this at all, so NC systematically overpays for its funded loans relative to what a calibrated model would bid.

Engine Competition Data

CNU and NC receive rich competition data from Engine, one of the largest subprime and near-prime marketplaces. This is an unusually complete view of competitor behavior.

What Engine provides
Data pointCustomers in scopeWhat we can do with it
All offers presented Customers we approved Direct competitor pricing comparison for our approved population
All offers presented Customers we rejected Understand what competitors offered to our rejections — coverage gap analysis
Funded loans Approved customers who chose a competitor Identify customers we could have won — the convertible-loss population
What makes Engine unusual
Most lenders see only their own funnel. Engine gives a window into competitor pricing and customer choices across the full marketplace, including for customers we approved but did not win.
The selection problem — covered in Section C
Engine data is conditioned on our own approve/reject decision. We never observe competitor funding for customers we rejected. Powerful for prediction on the approved population, but requires correction before using for policy decisions that change the approval boundary.

10 DS Areas in This Channel

The lead-acquisition channel has rich, structured data across funnels, auctions, and competition. Here is a map of how ten core data science areas apply to this specific context.

01 · Predictive Analytics
Conversion propensity modeling
Per-lead P(fund) using application, lead provider, and behavioral signals. CNU has a production model; NC does not. The highest-leverage input for CPF control.
02 · Gradient Boosting
Bid optimization and lead scoring
GBM models for replacing the NC score ladder with a calibrated E[LTV] x P(convert) bid. Requires mandatory calibration (Brier score, reliability curve) not just AUC.
03 · Survival Analysis
Clearing-price and verification completion
PingTree wins are right-censored: we won but do not know the minimum clearing price. Survival models estimate P(win | bid = x). Also applicable to predicting verification drop-off.
04 · Time Series and Forecasting
CPF forecasting and anomaly detection
Forecast next-week CPF per source and alert on drift using ARIMA, Prophet, or SPC. Replaces manual weekly monitoring and enables proactive source-pausing decisions.
05 · Causal Inference
Attribution and off-policy evaluation
Incrementality measurement for channel attribution. Doubly-robust estimators and IPW for evaluating bid policy changes without live experimentation on every variant.
06 · Clustering and Segmentation
Lead provider quality tiers and customer segments
Unsupervised segmentation of lead providers by quality, intent signal, and CPF profile. Separate bid models or multipliers per segment rather than a single population-level model.
07 · Graph Theory and Network Analysis
Affiliate ecosystem and traffic routing
Model the lead provider network as a graph where edges carry volume, quality, and CPF. Identify which affiliates act as hubs, which are redundant, and where traffic routing creates hidden correlation.
08 · Anomaly Detection
Attribution bugs and data quality monitoring
Turn the manual misattribution hunt into an automated classifier. Flag session/source patterns that historically signal misattribution without requiring manual review each cycle.
09 · Elasticity and Regression
Offer elasticity and price sensitivity
Estimate how conversion rate changes with offer terms and bid price using Engine competition data. Understand where competitors are price-sensitive versus terms-sensitive.
10 · Reinforcement Learning
Dynamic bidding with explore / exploit
Long-run: treat bidding as a bandit or RL problem where each bid is an action with a delayed reward signal (funding). Requires a validated offline policy evaluator before going live.

The Hard Problem: We Act on What We Observe

Every model in this channel shares the same underlying difficulty. Our actions select what we observe, and we want decisions that change those actions — which changes what we observe next.

The feedback loop
We place a bid (act) which determines which leads we win (what we observe) and we want to find a better bid (a new action) which changes the distribution of leads we win (what we observe next).
Where it shows up
ModelSelection problem
Conversion modelTrained on won leads only — P(fund) biased upward on the observed sample
Clearing-price modelWins are right-censored — we know we won but not the minimum price to win
Engine analysisConditioned on our own approve/reject — rejected-customer outcomes are invisible
Any policy backtestHistorical data was generated by a different policy, not the one being evaluated
Build order — de-risked
1

NC conversion model

Cleanest starting point. Binary label, existing training data, no live policy risk.

2

NC clearing-price model

Free labels on PingTree losses. Survival approach on right-censored wins.

3

NC bid optimization backtest

Offline policy evaluation combining 1 and 2. IPW or doubly-robust estimator before any live test.

4

Live exploration budget

Small randomized-bid component to generate unbiased training data. Requires stakeholder approval.

The Censored Data Problem

Engine data is powerful for prediction on what we observe. It is dangerous for policy decisions that change what we observe. Two sub-problems with different solutions.

Part 1 — Convertible loss prediction
Engine shows us approved customers who then funded with a competitor. The existing RCA described this population. The goal is to make it predictive: score each potential loss by P(competitor would fund this customer).
Tractable with standard ML. The label exists. The selection is manageable within the approved population.
Part 2 — Selection correction for policy
We never observe competitor funding for customers we rejected. Any Engine-trained model is conditioned on our approve/reject decision. Using it to set the approval boundary requires correcting for this selection.
Requires formal correction. Heckman two-stage selection model, inverse probability weighting, or control-function approach. Start with Heckman for interpretability.
The line that matters. Prediction on the observed distribution is standard ML. Valid inference for a decision that changes the distribution requires selection correction — because the decision changes who we approve, which changes who we observe.
